Acts · Chapter 1

Waiting for the Promise

The risen Christ departs — and leaves a mission in his wake

Verses 1–26 Date c. Spring AD 30 Persons 11 Locations 3 Events 9

After his resurrection, Jesus spends forty days with his followers, teaching them about God's kingdom and promising that the Holy Spirit will soon come upon them. He then ascends visibly into the sky from the Mount of Olives, leaving his disciples to return to Jerusalem, where they pray together and choose a twelfth apostle to replace Judas, who had betrayed Jesus and died.

Event 01 1:1–2

Prologue: A Letter to Theophilus

The author opens by reminding his reader Theophilus of the first volume — the Gospel of Luke — which recorded all that Jesus "began to do and teach." This volume picks up where that one ended, with Jesus giving final instructions to his apostles through the Holy Spirit before his ascension. The brief prologue establishes Acts as a deliberate continuation of a larger story about Jesus.

Event 02 1:3 Jerusalem

Forty Days of Resurrection Appearances

After his death, Jesus appeared alive to his apostles many times over a period of forty days, giving them convincing proofs that he was truly risen. During these appearances he taught them about the kingdom of God. The forty-day period deliberately echoes Moses on Sinai and Jesus's forty days in the wilderness, framing this time as a sacred period of divine preparation.

Event 03 1:4–5 Jerusalem

The Promise of the Holy Spirit

While eating with his disciples, Jesus gives them a direct command: do not leave Jerusalem, but wait for the gift the Father has promised — the Holy Spirit. Jesus contrasts John's water baptism with the coming baptism in the Holy Spirit, which he says will happen within days. This command transforms Jerusalem from merely the end of one story into the launching pad for another.

Event 04 1:6–8 Jerusalem

"Will You Now Restore the Kingdom?"

The apostles ask Jesus whether he is about to restore the political kingdom of Israel — a question that reveals they still expected a nationalistic liberation. Jesus redirects them: the timing of such things is not theirs to know, but they will receive power when the Holy Spirit comes on them. He then gives them their mission: to be his witnesses in Jerusalem, all Judea, Samaria, and to the ends of the earth — a geographic outline that maps the entire book of Acts.

Event 05 1:9–11 Mount of Olives

The Ascension of Jesus

As the disciples watch, Jesus is taken up and a cloud hides him from their sight. They stand gazing upward into the sky, until two men dressed in white appear and tell them that Jesus has gone to heaven but will return in the same way they saw him go. The Ascension marks the decisive transition from the era of Jesus's earthly presence to the era of the Spirit and the church.

Event 06 1:12 Mount of Olives → Jerusalem

The Walk Back to Jerusalem

After the Ascension, the eleven apostles walk back to Jerusalem from the Mount of Olives — a distance described as a Sabbath day's walk, roughly 900 metres. This brief note grounds the story geographically and shows the disciples obeying Jesus's instruction to stay in Jerusalem rather than returning home to Galilee.

Event 07 1:13–14 Upper Room

The Upper Room: United in Prayer

The eleven apostles go to the upper room of a house in Jerusalem, where they devote themselves continuously to prayer. They are joined by a larger group that includes the women who followed Jesus, Mary his mother, and his brothers. Luke names all eleven apostles, creating a formal roster that underlines their identity as the authorized witnesses of everything that follows.

Event 08 1:15–20 Upper Room

Peter Speaks: The Fate of Judas

With about 120 believers gathered, Peter stands and addresses them. He cites two psalms to argue that scripture predicted both Judas's betrayal and the need to replace him. He describes how Judas used the money from betraying Jesus to buy a field, where he died in a gruesome way, and that the field became known as Akeldama — "Field of Blood." Peter's interpretation of scripture shows the early community reading the Old Testament through the lens of Jesus.

Event 09 1:21–26 Upper Room

Matthias Chosen to Replace Judas

Peter outlines the qualifications for the replacement apostle: he must have accompanied Jesus from the baptism of John until the Ascension, and must be a witness to the resurrection. Two candidates emerge — Joseph Barsabbas and Matthias. The community prays, asking God to reveal his choice, then cast lots — an ancient method of discernment — and the lot falls to Matthias. He is added to the eleven, restoring the Twelve.

Key Persons

Jesus

Risen Lord

First mention: 1:1

Appears to his apostles over forty days after his resurrection, giving final instructions before ascending to heaven. His departure sets the entire story of Acts in motion.

Peter

Apostle, Leader

First mention: 1:13

Takes the lead in the Upper Room by addressing the group about replacing Judas. He cites scripture, outlines qualifications, and oversees the selection process.

The Eleven Apostles

Group — Apostles

First mention: 1:13

Witness the Ascension and return to Jerusalem together. They form the core leadership of the early church and will lead the community after Pentecost.

The Women

Group — Disciples

First mention: 1:14

Join the apostles in constant prayer in the Upper Room. Their presence signals that the early community included both men and women from the very beginning.

Mary, Mother of Jesus

Disciple

First mention: 1:14

Explicitly named among those gathered in the Upper Room in prayer. This is her final appearance in the New Testament narrative.

Brothers of Jesus

Group — Disciples

First mention: 1:14

Their presence in the prayer community marks a change of heart — they had been skeptical during Jesus's ministry. James will soon lead the Jerusalem church.

Matthias

Apostle — newly chosen

First mention: 1:23

Chosen by lot to fill the vacancy left by Judas. Had followed Jesus from the baptism of John all the way through the Ascension. His selection restores the Twelve.

Joseph Barsabbas

Disciple, candidate

First mention: 1:23

The other finalist considered to replace Judas. Though the lot falls to Matthias, his inclusion shows the wider community was large and well-qualified.

Judas Iscariot

Betrayer — referenced, deceased

First mention: 1:16

Betrayed Jesus and subsequently died in Akeldama, the Field of Blood. Peter recounts this story to explain why a replacement apostle is needed.

Two Men in White Robes

Angels — messengers

First mention: 1:10

Appear at the Ascension and promise Jesus will return. Their message redirects the disciples from gazing upward toward the mission ahead.

Theophilus

Addressee

First mention: 1:1

The book is dedicated to Theophilus — "friend of God" in Greek — the same recipient as Luke's Gospel. Acts is a sequel for this educated Greek-speaking reader.

Theological Themes

◆ The promise and power of the Holy Spirit
◆ Continuity: Acts as the sequel to the Gospel of Luke
◆ Witness: being sent to the ends of the earth
◆ Scriptural fulfillment and the interpretation of the Old Testament
◆ Community, prayer, and shared waiting
◆ Restoration of the Twelve as a symbolic act

Discussion Questions

  1. 1

    The disciples asked Jesus when he would restore the kingdom. In what ways do people today misunderstand what God's kingdom looks like? How does Jesus's answer redirect their expectations?

  2. 2

    The community spent ten days in prayer between the Ascension and Pentecost. What does this waiting period suggest about how God's purposes unfold, and how does it challenge our desire for immediate results?

  3. 3

    Peter quotes from the Psalms to make sense of Judas's death and the need for a replacement. What does this tell us about how the early church read their scriptures, and how does it shape the way you read the Old Testament?

  4. 4

    Matthias was chosen by lot after prayer. What does this method of decision-making say about the community's trust in God, and how do you think about making decisions in your own community?

  5. 5

    The "ends of the earth" in Acts 1:8 was a phrase that meant Rome or beyond. What is your personal "end of the earth" — the place or person farthest from your natural circle that the Spirit might be calling you toward?
